NATIONAL PARK


India boasts 106 national parks, which cover about 1.35% of the country’s total land area. These parks are vital for conserving India’s diverse wildlife, safeguarding endangered species, and maintaining natural habitats. From the snow-capped vistas of Hemis National Park in Ladakh to the lush mangroves of Sundarbans National Park in West Bengal, each park features distinct ecosystems. National parks such as Jim Corbett, Kaziranga, and Ranthambore are famous for their efforts in tiger conservation through Project Tiger. Recent additions like Dehing Patkai and Raimona in Assam highlight India’s ongoing dedication to wildlife protection. Additionally, these parks support ecotourism, scientific research, and environmental education, making them essential to India’s ecological and cultural legacy.

Ranthambore National Park – Sawai Madhopur Rajasthan It’ll blow your mind.

It is the biggest and the most famous wildlife conservation area of Rajasthan with a total area of 1334 sq. km. It became a national park on 1 November 1980 and today it attracts thousands of tourists from all over the world because of the exquisite sightings of Bengal tigers. Originally established as Sawai Madhopur Game Sanctuary in 1955, it came under the Project Tiger in 1973 and later subsumed two more adjoining sanctuaries which are Swai Mansingh sanctuary and Keladevi Sanctuary. Besides the tiger, visitors also get to see chital, mugger crocodile, rhesus macaque, southern plains gray langur, sloth bear, striped hyena, sambar, wild boar, nilgai, Indian leopard along with a broad array of reptiles, birds, plans, and trees.

Eravikulam National Park, Munnar See for yourself.

The famous Eravikulam National Park is the home of the rare and endangered Nilgiri Tahr, a type of mountain goat found in the Western Ghats! The park is 97 sq kilometers of Shola grasslands, forests, medicinal herbs, epiphytic orchids, and balsams sheltering animals like Nilgiri langurs, Indian bison, elephants, leopards, and even otters, along with birds like the Nilgiri flycatcher, white-bellied shortwing, and Nilgiri wood pigeons. The rare Neelakurinji flowers bloom here once every 12 years, turning the whole landscape purplish-blue! The Anamudi Peak, the highest peak in South India at an elevation of 2,695 meters, also makes it one of the best wildlife sanctuaries in India!

Keoladeo National Park, RajasthanCheckmate.

Also called Bharatpur Bird Sanctuary, Keoladeo National Park is a UNESCO World Heritage Site and among the most eminent bird breeding and feeding grounds in the world. It’s renowned for its rich biodiversity of aviaries, both endemic and migratory birds, that can be spotted near the man-made wetlands, thin forests, dry grasslands, and swamps inside the park. Keoladeo shelters thousands of migratory birds in the winter season, including the majestic and critically endangered Siberian Crane. It can only be spotted in this region in the winter. You can spot animals like Indian fox, nilgai, jackals, wild boar, pythons, and turtles along with over 370 bird species like storks, ibis, darters, cormorants, pelicans, herons, egrets, ducks, cranes, eagles, wagtails, kingfishers, robins, buzzards, and waders.
